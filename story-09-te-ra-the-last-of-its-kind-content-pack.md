# Week 9 Content Pack — "Te Rā: The Last of Its Kind" (Connected 2024, an interview by Hamish Ansley)

**Type:** Non-fiction (interview/Q&A format) | **Reading level:** Year 5/6
**Theme:** Te Rā, an extraordinary 200+ year old Māori woven sail — mātauranga Māori, science, and coming home
**Curriculum:** NZC English, Phase 2 (Years 4–6) — with strong Science and bicultural links

---

## Story Snapshot (for you)
Te Rā is probably the only Māori woven sail of its kind left in the world — made from harakeke (flax) in Aotearoa in the 1700s, and kept for most of the last 200 years in the British Museum in London, without anyone quite knowing how it got there. In 2019, three researchers — a weaver/scholar, an expert weaver, and a materials scientist — travelled to London to study Te Rā, combining traditional Māori weaving knowledge (mātauranga) with modern science (like polarised light microscopy) to understand how it was made and what it reveals about Māori voyaging technology. The interview also covers the emotional experience of studying and briefly bringing home a taonga that had been away from its people for so long.

**Nice link back to earlier weeks:** this continues the waka/sail technology thread from Weeks 2, 3, 5 and 7, and echoes Week 6's story of research, evidence, and the significance of bringing a taonga home.

## 🟦 MONDAY — Whole Class: Lead-in, Vocabulary & Comprehension

### Lead-in questions
1. If your family owned something really old and important, and it ended up far away in another country's museum for 200 years, how would you feel about it coming home?
2. What is something in your own family or culture that is treasured and passed down? Why does it matter?
3. Have you ever used both traditional knowledge (like a family recipe or skill) and modern science or tools together to understand or check something?

### Key vocabulary
| Word | Kid-friendly meaning |
|---|---|
| taonga | a treasured object of cultural, spiritual, or historical significance |
| mātauranga | traditional Māori knowledge |
| whakapapa | genealogy — the history and lineage of a person, family, or object |
| harakeke | the Māori word for flax, a plant used for weaving |
| raranga | the art of weaving |
| muka | fibre prepared from flax, used for cord or binding |
| wānanga | a gathering for learning and discussion |
| mauri | the spirit or life-force of a person, place, or object |
| polarised light microscope | a scientific tool used to examine fine details of materials like fibres |
| tūpuna | ancestors |

### Whole-class read
- Read as an interview, with different readers taking the parts of Donna, Rānui, and Catherine.

### Basic comprehension questions (literal — whole class)
1. What is Te Rā, and how old is it?
2. Where was Te Rā kept for most of the last 200 years?
3. What did the three researchers want to find out about Te Rā?
4. What scientific tool did they use, and what did it prove?
5. What might the kererū and kāhu feathers on the sail have been used for?
6. How did people feel when Te Rā was brought back to Aotearoa?

**Curriculum tags:** oral language • vocabulary knowledge • non-fiction interview text features • literal comprehension • bicultural perspectives (mātauranga Māori) • cross-curricular Science link (materials science, microscopy)

## 🟩 TUESDAY — Level Groups: Guided Reading

**Group A — needs more support**
- Re-read the interview Q&A with the teacher.
- Task: identify the three researchers and their roles (weaver/artist/scholar, expert weaver, scientist).

**Group B — at level (teacher-led guided group)**
- Focus: understanding how science and traditional knowledge worked together.
- Task: explain in your own words what the polarised light microscope revealed, and why proving the harakeke scientifically mattered alongside traditional weaving knowledge.

**Group C — extending**
- Focus: emotional and cultural significance.
- Task: discuss why not knowing Te Rā's full whakapapa (how it got to the British Museum) matters, and why bringing it home — even temporarily — was so emotionally significant. Compare this to Week 6's story about Te Pokohiwi.

**Curriculum tags:** reading progression — interview text structure, understanding scientific evidence • differentiated instruction • bicultural perspectives

## 🟨 WEDNESDAY — Pairs: Reading Activities

1. **Interview Feature Hunt** — Pairs write one new question they'd ask one of the three researchers, and a likely evidence-based answer.
2. **Vocabulary Match-Up** — Match key terms (taonga, mātauranga, harakeke, raranga, muka, wānanga, mauri) to their meanings.
3. **Evidence Detective** — Match each scientific/weaving clue (the microscope images, the zigzag weave pattern, the split feathers) to what it reveals about Te Rā.
4. **How Would It Feel?** — Pairs discuss and write about how it might feel to see or touch a 200-year-old taonga from your own culture for the first time.

**Curriculum tags:** non-fiction text features (interview structure) • comprehension strategies (evidence-matching) • collaborative talk • bicultural perspectives

## Christian Reflection — School Values
*Soli Deo Gloria — To God Alone the Glory*

| Value | Reflection question |
|---|---|
| **Aroha** (Love) | Donna described crying because she felt sorry for Te Rā being alone, away from its people for so long. How does this remind us that people — and even meaningful things — are made to belong and be loved within community, not to be alone? |
| **Mōhio** (Know) | The researchers combined careful science (the microscope) with traditional weaving knowledge to understand Te Rā fully. How is this like using both careful observation of creation and God's Word together to grow in knowing Him and His world? |
| **Manaaki** (Serve) | Donna, Rānui, and Catherine spent years using their skills and knowledge to serve their community by bringing understanding of Te Rā back home. How can we use our own skills to serve and preserve something important for others? |
| **Pānga** (Impact) | Even though Te Rā could only come home for a short time, its return had a huge impact — inspiring a child who saw it to want to make his own sail. How can something we do that seems small or temporary still leave a big, lasting impact on someone else? |
