# Week 7 Content Pack — "The Long Pause" (Connected, Level 3, by Dr Amber Aranui)

**Type:** Non-fiction (explanation text, multiple competing scientific theories) | **Reading level:** Year 6 (challenge text)
**Theme:** The 1,000-year gap between the Lapita migration and the settlement of East Polynesia/Aotearoa
**Curriculum:** NZC English, Phase 2 (Years 4–6) — with strong Science (archaeology, hypothesis-testing) links

---

## Story Snapshot (for you)
About 3,500 years ago, the Lapita people rapidly explored and settled West Polynesia (Fiji, Sāmoa, Tonga) in just 500 years. Then — mysteriously — exploration stopped for at least another 1,000 years, before people finally voyaged on to Hawai'i, Rapa Nui, and Aotearoa. This text presents **three competing scientific theories** for why: (1) canoe technology wasn't yet good enough for the longer, rougher East Polynesian crossings, (2) resource competition and possible droughts in West Polynesia may have pushed people to search for new land, and (3) computer models suggest wind patterns changed at just the right times to make eastward sailing possible. This is the most complex text of the term — a great one for teaching how scientists evaluate multiple, competing explanations rather than settling for a single simple answer.

**Nice link back to earlier weeks:** radiocarbon dating (Week 6), and waka technology/the waka hourua (Weeks 2, 3 and 5) all reappear here — worth pointing out explicitly.

## 🟦 MONDAY — Whole Class: Lead-in, Vocabulary & Comprehension

### Lead-in questions
1. Imagine sailing into the ocean with no idea what's on the other side, or if there's anything there at all. What would make you brave enough to go?
2. Why do you think people might stop exploring somewhere for a very long time, then suddenly start again?
3. What do you already know about how the Pacific Islands, including Aotearoa, were first settled?

### Key vocabulary
| Word | Kid-friendly meaning |
|---|---|
| Lapita | the early Pacific people who first migrated from the Bismarck Islands out into the Pacific |
| migration | the movement of people from one place to settle in another |
| archaeologist | a scientist who studies the past through objects and remains people left behind |
| hypothesis | a possible explanation that still needs more evidence to prove it |
| outrigger canoe | a canoe with a float attached to one side for balance, but limited in size and distance |
| waka hourua | a double-hulled canoe that could travel further and carry more |
| drought | a long period of very dry weather |
| computer models | computer programs that use maths and data to predict what might have happened |
| hospitable | pleasant and favourable for living in |
| radiocarbon dating | measuring radioactive carbon in remains to estimate how long ago something lived |

### Whole-class read
- This is a denser text with multiple theories — read section by section, pausing after each theory to summarise it in one sentence as a class.

### Basic comprehension questions (literal — whole class)
1. Who were the Lapita, and where did they first settle?
2. About how long was "the long pause" between the two migrations?
3. Name two possible reasons scientists suggest for the long pause.
4. What is the difference between an outrigger canoe and a waka hourua?
5. How do computer models help researchers understand wind patterns from the past?
6. Which was the last major area of the Pacific to be settled?

**Curriculum tags:** oral language • vocabulary knowledge • literal comprehension • bicultural/Aotearoa perspectives • cross-curricular Science link (hypothesis-testing, radiocarbon dating)

## 🟩 TUESDAY — Level Groups: Guided Reading

**Group A — needs more support**
- Re-read the timeline section with the teacher.
- Task: label a simple timeline with the key migration dates (Lapita arrive in Bismarck Islands → West Polynesia settled → the long pause → East Polynesia/Aotearoa settled).

**Group B — at level (teacher-led guided group)**
- Focus: explaining one theory clearly.
- Task: choose one of the three theories (technology, resources/drought, or wind patterns) and explain it in your own words, using evidence from the text.

**Group C — extending**
- Focus: evaluating multiple competing explanations.
- Task: compare all three theories. Which do you find most convincing, and why? Discuss why scientists test several hypotheses rather than settling for one simple answer.

**Curriculum tags:** reading progression — evaluating multiple explanations, technical vocabulary • differentiated instruction • bicultural perspectives

## 🟨 WEDNESDAY — Pairs: Reading Activities

1. **Theory Sorting** — Pairs sort evidence statements under the correct theory heading: Technology / Resources & Drought / Wind Patterns.
2. **Vocabulary Match-Up** — Match key terms (Lapita, hypothesis, outrigger canoe, waka hourua, drought, hospitable) to their meanings.
3. **Canoe Comparison** — Compare the outrigger canoe and the waka hourua using a table or Venn diagram — what could each one do?
4. **Which Theory Convinces You?** — Pairs discuss and write a short joint justification for which theory they find most convincing, using evidence from the text.

**Curriculum tags:** non-fiction text features (diagrams, timelines) • comprehension strategies (comparing and evaluating explanations) • collaborative talk • bicultural perspectives

## Christian Reflection — School Values
*Soli Deo Gloria — To God Alone the Glory*

| Value | Reflection question |
|---|---|
| **Aroha** (Love) | Over generations, people kept improving their boats and knowledge so that one day, exploration could continue — even though they might never see the results themselves. How can loving others sometimes mean patiently working on something now, even if we don't see the outcome ourselves? |
| **Mōhio** (Know) | Scientists don't just guess about the past — they carefully gather evidence and test different explanations before deciding what's most likely true. How is this similar to how we should search carefully for truth about God and His story, rather than jumping to conclusions? |
| **Manaaki** (Serve) | Over generations, people worked together — developing new tools, boats and knowledge — to serve those who would come after them on this incredible journey. How can we use our own gifts to serve and prepare the way for people who come after us? |
| **Pānga** (Impact) | The eventual voyages to Aotearoa changed the future of an entire country forever. How can small, patient steps we take now end up having a huge impact later, even if we can't see it yet? |
