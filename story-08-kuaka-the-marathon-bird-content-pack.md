# Week 8 Content Pack — "Kūaka: The Marathon Bird" (School Journal)

**Type:** Non-fiction (illustrated magazine-style article) | **Reading level:** Year 5/6
**Theme:** The bar-tailed godwit's incredible non-stop migration, and both scientific and traditional Māori ways of understanding it
**Curriculum:** NZC English, Phase 2 (Years 4–6) — with strong Science and bicultural links

---

## Story Snapshot (for you)
Kūaka (bar-tailed godwits) make one of the most extraordinary journeys in the animal kingdom — flying non-stop between Alaska and New Zealand, a distance of around 11,000 km, without landing to rest, eat, or drink. Before departure their bodies transform: they fatten up dramatically and some of their internal organs shrink to save weight, then regrow once they arrive. Modern satellite tracking has revealed the details of this journey scientifically, while Māori traditional knowledge (maramataka, the lunar calendar, and whakataukī, traditional proverbs) has understood and marked the kūaka's arrival and departure as a seasonal signal for many generations. This is a great, uplifting text after some heavier weeks — genuine wonder at what creation can do, alongside real science.

**Nice link back to earlier weeks:** this connects naturally to Week 1 (the fictional godwit story, *The Journey*) and continues the term's Science/bicultural threads from Weeks 6 and 7.

## 🟦 MONDAY — Whole Class: Lead-in, Vocabulary & Comprehension

### Lead-in questions
1. Could you run for 8–9 days without stopping to eat, drink, or rest? How do you think a bird could fly that long, non-stop?
2. What signs in nature (birds arriving, plants flowering) tell people that a new season is coming?
3. What do you already know about the kūaka/godwit and its incredible journey? (A nice link back to Week 1's story, *The Journey*.)

### Key vocabulary
| Word | Kid-friendly meaning |
|---|---|
| kūaka | the Māori name for the bar-tailed godwit, a large migratory shorebird |
| migratory | travelling regularly between two places, usually with the seasons |
| non-stop | without any pause or rest along the way |
| satellite tracking | using satellites to follow and record an animal's exact movements |
| estuary | where a river meets the sea, often rich in food for wading birds |
| maramataka | the traditional Māori lunar calendar |
| whakataukī | a Māori proverb or saying that carries traditional wisdom |
| V formation | a flying pattern where birds take turns leading to save energy |
| fuel up / fatten up | eating heavily to store enough energy for a long journey |
| organs | internal body parts (like the stomach or liver) that carry out life functions |

### Whole-class read
- Read the article together, pausing at photos/diagrams of the migration route and body changes.

### Basic comprehension questions (literal — whole class)
1. What is a kūaka, and where does it migrate between?
2. About how far do kūaka fly, and how long does the journey take?
3. What happens to a kūaka's body before it begins its migration?
4. How do scientists track where kūaka go?
5. What role does the kūaka play in traditional Māori knowledge of the seasons?
6. Why do kūaka fly in a V formation?

**Curriculum tags:** oral language • vocabulary knowledge • literal comprehension • bicultural perspectives (maramataka, whakataukī) • cross-curricular Science link (adaptation, migration, tracking technology)

## 🟩 TUESDAY — Level Groups: Guided Reading

**Group A — needs more support**
- Re-read the "getting ready to fly" section with the teacher.
- Task: sequence the stages of a kūaka's migration preparation (fatten up → organs shrink → depart → fly non-stop → arrive and refuel → organs regrow).

**Group B — at level (teacher-led guided group)**
- Focus: understanding scientific tracking.
- Task: explain in your own words how satellite tracking works and what scientists have learned from it; find evidence of the physical toll the journey takes on the bird's body.

**Group C — extending**
- Focus: valuing two different ways of knowing.
- Task: discuss why both Māori traditional knowledge (whakataukī, maramataka) and modern satellite science matter for understanding kūaka — two different, valuable ways of knowing the same bird. Connect this to Weeks 6 and 7's science themes.

**Curriculum tags:** reading progression — sequencing, understanding scientific processes • differentiated instruction • bicultural perspectives

## 🟨 WEDNESDAY — Pairs: Reading Activities

1. **Body Changes Sequencing** — Pairs sequence cards showing the stages of a kūaka's body before, during, and after migration.
2. **Vocabulary Match-Up** — Match key terms (kūaka, migratory, satellite tracking, maramataka, whakataukī, V formation) to their meanings.
3. **V-Formation Discussion** — Pairs discuss and sketch why flying in a V formation might save energy for a flock of birds.
4. **Two Ways of Knowing** — Pairs match pieces of evidence to whether they come from traditional Māori knowledge or modern scientific tracking — and discuss why both matter.

**Curriculum tags:** non-fiction text features (diagrams, photos) • comprehension strategies (sequencing, comparing sources of knowledge) • collaborative talk • bicultural perspectives

| Value | Reflection question |
|---|---|
| **Aroha** (Love) | The kūaka take turns leading the flock in a V formation, sharing the hardest work so every bird can complete the journey together. How does this remind us of God's call to love others by sharing burdens and looking out for one another? |
