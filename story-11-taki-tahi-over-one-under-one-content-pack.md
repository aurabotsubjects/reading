# Week 11 Content Pack — "Taki Tahi | Over One, Under One" (Connected 2024, a poem by Briar Grace-Smith)

**Type:** Poetry | **Reading level:** Year 5/6
**Theme:** Weaving a waka's sail together — the rhythm of shared, skilled work, and the sail's first test at sea
**Curriculum:** NZC English, Phase 2 (Years 4–6)

> **A note on this text:** this is a poem, so in line with copyright practice, this pack describes its content, structure and devices rather than quoting lines directly. Students will read the actual poem from their own copy of the text — the activities below ask them to find and discuss specific lines themselves.

---

## Story Snapshot (for you)
This poem describes a group of weavers making a waka's sail from harakeke, working through the "taki tahi" pattern — over one, under one — a phrase that repeats through the poem like the rhythm of the weaving itself. While their hands work, the weavers chat about everyday things (sport, television) right alongside traditional stories and atua (gods) — showing that this skilled, sacred work is also warm and ordinary, done side by side. The poem follows the sail from its finishing, through a quiet moment of pride, to the moment it's finally raised and tested against the wind — and passes the test, powering the waka out across the sea.

**Term capstone:** this is the third and final text from the same journal collection as Weeks 9 and 10 — *Te Rā* (a real interview about an ancient sail) and *Tahu and the Dancing Feathers* (a fictional story about a sail being made). All three texts explore weaving and waka sails through a different genre: non-fiction, fiction, and poetry. This is a great week to draw the whole collection — and arguably the whole term's voyaging theme — together.

## 🟦 MONDAY — Whole Class: Lead-in, Vocabulary & Comprehension

### Lead-in questions
1. Have you ever done a repetitive job (like kneading dough, or a chore) while chatting with friends or family about everyday things? What did you talk about?
2. Poems often repeat a phrase or line again and again. Why might a poet choose to do that?
3. What do you already know about weaving a waka's sail? (Remember Te Rā and Tahu and the Dancing Feathers!)

### Key vocabulary
| Word | Kid-friendly meaning |
|---|---|
| taki tahi | the "over one, under one" weaving pattern used in raranga |
| refrain | a repeated line or phrase in a poem, often for rhythm or emphasis |
| simile | a comparison using "like" or "as" |
| metaphor | a comparison that says one thing IS another, without using "like" or "as" |
| imagery | descriptive language that helps the reader picture, hear, or feel something |
| ara | a path, way, or pattern |
| Tāwhirimātea | the atua (god) of wind and storms |
| karakia | a prayer or ritual chant |
| "Me inoi tātou" | a phrase meaning "let us pray" — an invitation to karakia |
| kaiwhatu / kairaranga | a weaver — a person who weaves |

### Whole-class read
- Read the poem aloud slowly, letting the repeated refrain build a rhythm — perhaps read it once through for the story, then again noticing the repetition.

### Basic comprehension questions (literal — whole class)
1. What are the weavers making in this poem?
2. What everyday things do the weavers talk about while they work?
3. What traditional stories or figures do the weavers also talk about?
4. What repeated phrase runs through the poem, describing the weaving pattern?
5. What happens to the sail at the end of the poem?
6. Which atua (god) is mentioned in connection with the wind testing the sail?

**Curriculum tags:** oral language • vocabulary knowledge (te reo Māori and poetic terms) • literal comprehension • bicultural perspectives • poetic form and devices

## 🟩 TUESDAY — Level Groups: Guided Reading

**Group A — needs more support**
- Re-read the poem with the teacher, section by section.
- Task: identify the repeated refrain in your own copy of the poem, and count how many times it appears. Discuss what's happening at each stage (starting to weave → chatting → finishing → raising the sail → sailing).

**Group B — at level (teacher-led guided group)**
- Focus: identifying poetic devices.
- Task: find one simile and one metaphor in your own copy of the poem. Copy each one down, then explain in your own words what is being compared.

**Group C — extending**
- Focus: interpreting meaning and connecting texts.
- Task: discuss why the poet includes everyday chatter (sport, television) alongside sacred, traditional stories (Māui, gods). What does that tell us about weaving as both ordinary and special? Connect this poem's message to *Te Rā* and *Tahu and the Dancing Feathers*.

**Curriculum tags:** reading progression — identifying poetic devices, interpreting meaning • differentiated instruction • bicultural perspectives

## 🟨 WEDNESDAY — Pairs: Reading Activities

1. **Refrain Hunt** — Pairs find and count the repeated refrain in the poem and discuss the effect it creates (rhythm, a sense of the steady weaving pattern).
2. **Vocabulary Match-Up** — Match key terms (taki tahi, refrain, simile, metaphor, ara, Tāwhirimātea, karakia) to their meanings.
3. **Simile & Metaphor Detective** — Pairs find and copy down one simile and one metaphor from the poem, then explain what's being compared and why it's effective.
4. **Three Texts, One Story** — Pairs compare all three texts in the collection (*Te Rā*, *Tahu and the Dancing Feathers*, and this poem) — what does each one contribute to understanding waka sails and weaving?

**Curriculum tags:** poetic devices (simile, metaphor, refrain) • comprehension strategies (comparing texts) • collaborative talk • bicultural perspectives

## Christian Reflection — School Values
*Soli Deo Gloria — To God Alone the Glory*

| Value | Reflection question |
|---|---|
| **Aroha** (Love) | The weavers work side by side, sharing both everyday chatter and treasured stories while making something beautiful together. How does working alongside others in love and friendship, like the weavers do, reflect how God wants us to live in community? |
| **Mōhio** (Know) | The refrain "over one, under one" repeats throughout the poem, like a steady rhythm the weavers can trust. How is returning again and again to God's Word and story like this steady, trustworthy rhythm in our own lives? |
| **Manaaki** (Serve) | Each weaver serves the group by focusing on their part of the pattern, patiently and carefully, so the whole sail comes together. How can we serve our own family, class, or team with the same patience and care? |
| **Pānga** (Impact) | The finished sail carries the waka safely across the wild sea, its purpose fulfilled. As we finish this term's reading journey together, what impact do you hope the things you've learned will have on your own life going forward? |
