# Week 10 Content Pack — "Tahu and the Dancing Feathers" (Connected 2024, by André Ngāpō)

**Type:** Fiction (narrative) | **Reading level:** Year 5/6
**Theme:** Patience, the value of small contributions, and understanding a sail's feathers
**Curriculum:** NZC English, Phase 2 (Years 4–6)

---

## Story Snapshot (for you)
Tahu desperately wants to help his sister Huia and the other weavers make a new sail, Hine-Rangimārie, to replace the old, worn-out Hinetakitaki. Instead, Whaea Pere gives him the job of splitting kererū feathers with a mussel shell — a job Tahu finds boring and unimportant compared to weaving. When he's told he's still too young to weave, he storms off, calling the feathers "useless." But when the new sail is finally raised and Tahu watches the feathers tremble and dance in the wind, he suddenly understands: the feathers show which way the wind blows, and his "small" job was essential all along.

**Nice link back to Week 9:** this fictional story explains the exact real detail from *Te Rā* — the kererū and kāhu feathers along a sail's edge showing wind direction. Pairing the two texts (fiction and real interview) is a great comprehension/synthesis opportunity.

## 🟦 MONDAY — Whole Class: Lead-in, Vocabulary & Comprehension

### Lead-in questions
1. Have you ever really wanted to help with something, but been told you were too young or not ready yet? How did that feel?
2. Do you think even small, boring-seeming jobs can be important? Can you think of an example?
3. What do you already know about how sails were made from harakeke (flax)? (Remember Te Rā from Week 9!)

### Key vocabulary
| Word | Kid-friendly meaning |
|---|---|
| raranga | weaving |
| karakia | a prayer or ritual chant |
| atua | a god or deity — e.g. Tāwhirimātea, atua of the wind |
| e moko | an affectionate term for a grandchild |
| koro | grandfather, or a respected male elder |
| whaea | aunty, or a respected female elder/teacher |
| ātaahua | beautiful |
| kūtai | mussel (used here as a cutting tool) |
| whanaunga | relatives |
| huru manu | feathers along the edge of a sail (recap from Week 9's Te Rā!) |

### Whole-class read
- Read the story together, noticing how Tahu's feelings shift from frustration to pride and understanding.

### Basic comprehension questions (literal — whole class)
1. What job does Whaea Pere give Tahu while the others weave the new sail?
2. Why does Tahu want to help weave the sail instead?
3. What is the name of the old sail, and what is happening to it?
4. What is the new sail called, and what does Koro Hotu say it will do?
5. Why does Tahu get upset and knock over the kete of feathers?
6. What does Tahu finally realise about the feathers at the end of the story?

**Curriculum tags:** oral language • vocabulary knowledge (te reo Māori) • literal comprehension • bicultural perspectives

## 🟩 TUESDAY — Level Groups: Guided Reading

**Group A — needs more support**
- Re-read the story with the teacher.
- Task: sequence 6 key events in order (the waka returns → Tahu watches the weavers → Tahu splits feathers → Tahu asks to help weave → the new sail is raised → Tahu understands the feathers' purpose).

**Group B — at level (teacher-led guided group)**
- Focus: tracking a character's changing feelings.
- Task: find evidence of Tahu's feelings changing from frustration to pride and understanding; explain what Whaea Pere means by "each job, big and small, helped make the sail."

**Group C — extending**
- Focus: connecting texts.
- Task: discuss the theme of patience and valuing different contributions. Connect this story's feathers to Te Rā's huru manu from Week 9 — this story explains *why* those feathers matter.

**Curriculum tags:** reading progression — tracking character development, inference • differentiated instruction • bicultural perspectives

## 🟨 WEDNESDAY — Pairs: Reading Activities

1. **Feather-Splitting Hot Seat** — One partner is Tahu, the other is Whaea Pere; re-enact the conversation about wanting to help weave.
2. **Vocabulary Match-Up** — Match key terms (raranga, karakia, atua, koro, whaea, kūtai, whanaunga) to their meanings.
3. **"Every Job Matters" Discussion** — Pairs discuss and write about a time a "small" job turned out to be more important than it seemed.
4. **Comparing Texts** — Pairs compare *Tahu and the Dancing Feathers* (fiction) with *Te Rā* (non-fiction interview) — what's similar, what's different, about how they present sails and feathers?

**Curriculum tags:** comprehension strategies (inference, comparing texts) • collaborative talk • bicultural perspectives

## Christian Reflection — School Values
*Soli Deo Gloria — To God Alone the Glory*

| Value | Reflection question |
|---|---|
| **Aroha** (Love) | Whaea Pere and Koro treat Tahu with patience and kindness even when he's frustrated and knocks over the kete. How can we show love and patience to someone who is struggling with disappointment or impatience, like Tahu? |
| **Mōhio** (Know) | Tahu didn't understand the purpose of his job until he saw the finished sail in action. How is this like trusting that God has a good purpose for the small or hard tasks He gives us, even when we can't see the full picture yet? |
| **Manaaki** (Serve) | Even though splitting feathers felt small and unimportant to Tahu, it was a real act of service that helped the whole sail succeed. How can we serve faithfully in small tasks, trusting that they matter, even when we don't feel important? |
| **Pānga** (Impact) | Tahu's carefully split feathers ended up being an essential part of guiding the waka safely across the sea. How can small, faithful actions we do now end up making a real difference later, even if we don't realise it at the time? |
